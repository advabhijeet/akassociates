Published May 2026 Property Due Diligence Checklist

Property purchase due diligence should begin before token money or an agreement to sell is signed. A clean title search is not limited to one sale deed; it usually requires checking the ownership chain, seller capacity, encumbrances, possession, mutation, tax records, litigation history and property-specific permissions.

1. Why Title Search Matters Before Purchase

Buying property without title verification can lead to disputes over ownership, possession, boundary, unpaid dues, mortgage, family claims, forged documents or pending litigation. 2. Core Documents To Check In Every State

  • Current sale deed, gift deed, partition deed, conveyance deed, lease deed or other transfer instrument.
  • Previous title-chain documents showing how ownership moved from earlier owners to the present seller.
  • Mutation, revenue, municipal or property-tax records showing administrative history.
  • Encumbrance certificate or available registration-search record, depending on the state system.

3. Seller-Side Capacity And Authority

Title search is incomplete without checking whether the seller is legally competent to sell. If the seller is an individual, verify identity, inheritance papers and co-owner consent where relevant. If the seller is a company, firm, society, trust or developer, check authority documents and signing powers.

4. Revenue, Municipal And Possession Records

Revenue and mutation entries can be useful, but they should not be treated as a complete substitute for title-chain documents. They often show possession or administrative record updates, while ownership may still depend on registered instruments, inheritance documents, court orders and other legal papers.

5. Encumbrance, Mortgage And Litigation Checks

Before purchase, check whether the property is mortgaged, attached, restrained by court order, involved in partition or injunction proceedings, subject to acquisition, affected by a developer dispute, or burdened with unpaid dues.

6. State-Wise Record Examples

State systems and record names differ. The following examples are indicative and must be adapted to the property type, local office practice and current online-record availability.

Bihar

Sale deed chain, mutation, jamabandi, khata, khesra, rent receipt, LPC where applicable, municipal holding/tax records and pending-case or partition documents.

Uttar Pradesh

Registered deed chain, khatauni, khasra, mutation record, revenue map, registry search, authority approval and possession/tax records depending on land or urban property type.

Delhi

Sale deed or conveyance deed, chain documents, DDA/L&DO/MCD or society records depending on the property type, mutation, tax records and possession documents.

Maharashtra

Sale deed chain, 7/12 extract, property card, mutation entries, index-II, society share certificate and no-dues papers where relevant.

7. Red Flags Before Purchase

  • The seller cannot provide the original title documents or gives inconsistent explanations.
  • The property is in possession of someone other than the seller without a clear written basis.
  • Mutation or tax records do not match the title-chain documents.
  • There are family disputes, pending partition issues or unclear legal-heir consent.

8. Buyer Enquiry Checklist

Before asking for legal review, prepare a short note with property location, property type, seller name, proposed purchase amount, available title documents, possession status, pending loan details, family/co-owner position and whether any agreement to sell or token receipt has already been signed.

9. Official References

Official record systems vary by state. For general statutory reference, readers may check the Transfer of Property Act, 1882 and Registration Act, 1908 on India Code, and relevant state land-record portals for current revenue or municipal records.
