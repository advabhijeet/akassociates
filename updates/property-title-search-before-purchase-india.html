Property Title Search Before Purchase in India: Document Checklist and State-Wise Records

A practical guide to the records a buyer should collect before purchasing immovable property, including title-chain papers, revenue records, municipal records, encumbrance checks, litigation searches and state-wise document examples.

Legal Insight
Published May 2026 Property Due Diligence Checklist

Property purchase due diligence should begin before token money or an agreement to sell is signed. A clean title search is not limited to one sale deed; it usually requires checking the ownership chain, seller capacity, encumbrances, possession, mutation, tax records, litigation history and property-specific permissions.

1. Why Title Search Matters Before Purchase

Buying property without title verification can lead to disputes over ownership, possession, boundary, unpaid dues, mortgage, family claims, forged documents or pending litigation. A title search helps the buyer understand whether the seller appears to have the legal ability to transfer the property and whether the property is affected by visible legal risks.

The exact records differ by state and property type. Agricultural land, urban flats, builder floors, society flats, leasehold properties and inherited properties may require different supporting documents.

2. Core Documents To Check In Every State

  • Current sale deed, gift deed, partition deed, conveyance deed, lease deed or other transfer instrument.
  • Previous title-chain documents showing how ownership moved from earlier owners to the present seller.
  • Mutation, revenue, municipal or property-tax records showing administrative history.
  • Encumbrance certificate or available registration-search record, depending on the state system.
  • Possession documents, site photographs, electricity/water/tax records and possession handover papers.
  • Identity, authority and capacity documents of the seller, including power of attorney, board resolution or succession papers where relevant.

3. Seller-Side Capacity And Authority

Title search is incomplete without checking whether the seller is legally competent to sell. If the seller is an individual, verify identity, marital/family context where relevant, inheritance papers and whether any co-owner consent is required. If the seller is a company, firm, society, trust or developer, check authority documents and signing powers.

For inherited property, review the death certificate, succession-related papers, family settlement, partition documents, probate or letters of administration where applicable, and whether all legal heirs or co-sharers are properly represented.

4. Revenue, Municipal And Possession Records

Revenue and mutation entries can be useful, but they should not be treated as a complete substitute for title-chain documents. They often show possession or administrative record updates, while ownership may still depend on registered instruments, inheritance documents, court orders and other legal papers.

Urban property may also require municipal mutation, property tax record, building sanction, completion or occupancy certificate, society no-dues certificate, maintenance records and electricity or water connection records.

5. Encumbrance, Mortgage And Litigation Checks

Before purchase, check whether the property is mortgaged, attached, restrained by court order, involved in partition or injunction proceedings, subject to acquisition, affected by a developer dispute, or burdened with unpaid dues. Bank loan closure letters, original title-deed custody, CERSAI records where relevant, court-case searches and registration records can be important.

Where the property is under a loan, the buyer should not rely only on verbal assurance that the loan will be cleared. The transaction structure should clearly handle repayment, release of original title documents, no-dues confirmation and registration timing.

6. State-Wise Record Examples

State systems and record names differ. The following examples are indicative and must be adapted to the property type, local office practice and current online-record availability.

Bihar

Sale deed chain, mutation, jamabandi, khata, khesra, rent receipt, LPC where applicable, municipal holding/tax records and pending-case or partition documents.

Uttar Pradesh

Registered deed chain, khatauni, khasra, mutation record, revenue map, registry search, authority approval and possession/tax records depending on land or urban property type.

Delhi

Sale deed or conveyance deed, chain documents, DDA/L&DO/MCD or society records depending on the property type, mutation, tax records, sanction/occupancy papers and possession documents.

Haryana

Sale deed chain, jamabandi, mutation, khasra girdawari, registration search, CLU or development permissions where relevant, tax records and authority/society documents.

Jharkhand

Registered deed chain, khatiyan, register-II, mutation, rent receipts, plot and holding records, local revenue records and any inheritance or partition papers.

Maharashtra

Sale deed chain, 7/12 extract, property card, mutation entries, index-II, society share certificate and no-dues papers where relevant, plus municipal and development permissions.

7. Red Flags Before Purchase

  • The seller cannot provide the original title documents or gives inconsistent explanations.
  • The property is in possession of someone other than the seller without a clear written basis.
  • Mutation or tax records do not match the title-chain documents.
  • There are family disputes, pending partition issues or unclear legal-heir consent.
  • The property is mortgaged, attached, under acquisition notice or subject to an injunction/status quo order.
  • The seller pressures the buyer to pay token money before document review.

8. Buyer Enquiry Checklist

Before asking for legal review, prepare a short note with property location, property type, seller name, proposed purchase amount, available title documents, possession status, pending loan details, family/co-owner position and whether any agreement to sell or token receipt has already been signed.

Attach the latest title deed, previous chain documents, mutation/revenue records, tax records, site photographs, seller identity/authority papers and any correspondence with the broker, developer, bank or seller.
