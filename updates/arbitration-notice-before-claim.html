Arbitration Notice Before Claim: Clause and Document Checklist

Before invoking arbitration or responding to an arbitration notice, the contract, dispute record, limitation dates and appointment procedure should be arranged carefully.

Why The Notice Stage Matters

Arbitration usually begins with a written invocation or notice based on an arbitration clause. The notice stage can affect the framing of disputes, appointment of arbitrator, limitation objections, interim relief strategy and settlement scope.

Before issuing or responding to a notice, identify the exact contract, arbitration clause, seat, venue, governing law, appointment mechanism, pre-arbitration steps and the relief proposed to be claimed.

A notice should not be treated as a formality. It may become the first formal record of the disputes referred to arbitration, the amount claimed, the appointment proposal and the timeline relied upon. A vague notice can create avoidable objections later.

Documents To Preserve

Contract And Clause

  • Main contract and annexures.
  • Arbitration clause.
  • Jurisdiction, seat, venue and governing law terms.
  • Amendments, extensions and side letters.

Dispute Record

  • Invoices, work records or performance documents.
  • Emails, letters, WhatsApp messages and meeting notes.
  • Notices, replies and settlement communications.
  • Admission, breach or termination records.

Claim Preparation

  • Claim amount and calculation sheet.
  • Timeline of breach and communications.
  • Evidence of loss, payment or performance.
  • Interim relief documents, if urgent protection is needed.
Key Questions Before Invoking Arbitration

Check whether the arbitration clause is valid and covers the dispute, whether pre-arbitration negotiation or notice steps are required, whether limitation is preserved, whether the claim calculation is supported, and whether interim protection may be required before or during arbitration.

For response-side matters, check whether the notice follows the contract, whether the appointment method is correct, whether the claim is time-barred, whether the dispute is arbitrable, and whether jurisdiction, seat or venue objections need to be raised at the correct stage.

Where several contracts or purchase orders exist, identify which clause applies to which transaction. If there are different dispute-resolution clauses, the notice should avoid assuming that all claims automatically travel through the same procedure.

Response And Settlement Considerations

A party receiving an arbitration notice should preserve the envelope, email headers, service proof and date of receipt. The response may need to deal with the claim, limitation, appointment mechanism, jurisdiction, settlement possibility and whether urgent counter-steps are needed.

Settlement discussions can continue, but they should be recorded carefully. If payment, performance, revised schedule or withdrawal terms are discussed, the terms should be clear about amount, dates, default consequences and whether arbitration is paused or continues.

First Enquiry Format

A useful first enquiry may include contract date, arbitration clause text, dispute amount, breach date, notice date, seat or venue, appointment status, current stage and whether urgent interim protection is required.

For a proposed notice, prepare a claim chart and the supporting documents. For a received notice, prepare a response note identifying admitted facts, disputed facts, limitation concerns, clause objections, payment record and any counterclaim position.
