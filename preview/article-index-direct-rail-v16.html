Property purchase due diligence should begin before token money or an agreement to sell is signed. A clean title search is not limited to one sale deed; it usually requires checking the ownership chain, seller capacity, encumbrances, possession, mutation, tax records, litigation history and property-specific permissions.

1. Why Title Search Matters Before Purchase

Buying property without title verification can lead to disputes over ownership, possession, boundary, unpaid dues, mortgage, family claims, forged documents or pending litigation.

2. Core Documents To Check In Every State

  • Current sale deed, gift deed, partition deed, conveyance deed, lease deed or other transfer instrument.
  • Previous title-chain documents showing ownership movement.
  • Mutation, revenue, municipal or property-tax records.
  • Encumbrance certificate or registration-search record where available.

Practical note: document names differ by state, but the checklist should remain structured enough for a buyer to collect papers before legal review.

3. Seller-Side Capacity And Authority

Title search is incomplete without checking whether the seller is legally competent to sell. Individual, company, trust, society, developer and inherited-property transactions require different authority records.

If the property is inherited, review death certificate, succession-related papers, family settlement, partition documents, probate or letters of administration where applicable.

4. Revenue, Municipal And Possession Records

Revenue and mutation entries can be useful, but they should not be treated as a complete substitute for title-chain documents.

Possession, property tax, municipal holding and electricity records may support factual verification, but ownership should still be traced from registered instruments and legally relevant orders.

5. Encumbrance, Mortgage And Litigation Checks

Before purchase, check whether the property is mortgaged, attached, restrained by court order, involved in partition or injunction proceedings, subject to acquisition, or burdened with unpaid dues.

Bank loan closure letters, original title-deed custody, CERSAI records where relevant, court-case searches and registration records can be important.

6. State-Wise Record Examples

Bihar

Sale deed chain, jamabandi, khata, khesra, mutation and rent receipts.

Delhi

Sale deed, chain documents, DDA/MCD or society records depending on property type.

Uttar Pradesh

Khatauni, khasra, mutation, registry search and authority records.

Maharashtra

7/12 extract, property card, index-II, mutation entries and society records.

7. Red Flags Before Purchase

  • The seller cannot provide original title documents.
  • Possession and title records do not match.
  • Mutation or tax records conflict with the sale deed chain.
  • There are unclear heirship, family or co-owner issues.

8. Buyer Enquiry Checklist

Prepare a short note with property location, seller details, property type, available title papers, possession status, pending loan details and current transaction stage.

Document checklists before sending an enquiry can reduce unnecessary back-and-forth and help a lawyer understand the transaction faster.

9. Official References

Official record systems vary by state. General statutory references may include the Transfer of Property Act, 1882 and Registration Act, 1908.
