Arbitration Clause Checklist Before A Dispute

Arbitration clauses often look short but carry important procedural consequences. Before sending a notice or drafting a claim, it helps to read the clause with a checklist instead of only for the word “arbitration.”

Clause Review

An arbitration clause should be read before the notice stage, not after a dispute file becomes urgent. Seat, venue, appointment method and notice wording can shape later questions about interim relief, challenge and enforcement.

1. Read The Clause Before The Notice

Before invoking arbitration or replying to an invocation notice, review the exact clause language. Important words often relate to seat, venue, governing law, institutional rules, number of arbitrators, language, timelines and whether any negotiation or escalation step is expected first.

2. Distinguish Seat, Venue And Appointment

The clause should be checked for the legal seat, the hearing venue, and the appointment mechanism. Those terms are not always interchangeable. Preserve the exact wording and any amendment, because later procedural questions may depend on these details.

3. Notice And Interim Relief Questions

Some disputes require an invocation notice before a claim is framed. Others may also raise an interim protection issue involving assets, performance, encashment, possession or records. Check the clause and the dispute stage before assuming that only one procedural route is available.

4. Preserve The Dispute Record

Keep the contract, amendments, invoices, account statements, correspondence, breach notices, meeting minutes, performance records, termination letters and settlement communications together. A clause review without the dispute file often misses practical issues.

5. Think Beyond Invocation

The clause should also be read with the later stages in mind. Appointment disputes, tribunal constitution, award challenge and enforcement may all turn on how the clause is framed and how the notice stage is handled.

6. First Enquiry Format

A useful first enquiry may include the contract date, exact arbitration clause text, seat or venue wording, dispute amount, present stage, whether notice has already been sent, and whether urgent interim protection or post-award planning is involved.
